Contrastive Loss

used to train a model to pull the digital representations of similar data points closer together in space while pushing dissimilar ones farther apart

by Charles Iliya Krempeaux

Contrastive Loss is a loss function and objective function used in artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML).

Problem (Images)

Here is the problem trying to be solved by contrastive loss.

Imagine we are dealing photo images.

Our goal would be to make it so that similar images end up close together, and differnt images end up further apart.

Problem (Audio)

If, however, we were are dealing with audio from people speaking, then the problem trying to be solved by contrastive loss would be —

Our goal wold be to make it so that speech from the same person end up close together, while speach from different people end up further apart.

Appoach

Let's give some more details to make what is going on more obvious.

Imagine we are dealing with 100x100 black-and-white photo images.

Most models want their input to be a vector, so we can turn these 100x100 image into a 10,000 dimensional vector. Because 10,000 = 100 x 100. We just need to create some convention with mapping the pixels in the image to a vector. For example:

  • pixel (0,0) in the image goes to position 0 of the vector.
  • pixel (1,0) in the image goes to position 1 of the vector.
  • pixel (2,0) in the image goes to position 2 of the vector.
  • …
  • pixel (99,0) in the image goes to position 99 of the vector.
  • pixel (0,1) in the image goes to position 100 of the vector.
  • pixel (1,1) in the image goes to position 101 of the vector.
  • pixel (2,1) in the image goes to position 102 of the vector.
  • pixel (3,1) in the image goes to position 103 of the vector.
  • …
  • pixel (99,1) in the image goes to position 199 of the vector.
  • pixel (0,2) in the image goes to position 200 of the vector.
  • pixel (1,2) in the image goes to position 201 of the vector.
  • pixel (2,2) in the image goes to position 202 of the vector.
  • … … …
  • pixel (98,99) in the image goes to position 9,998 of the vector.
  • pixel (99,99) in the image goes to position 9,999 of the vector.

We could do this image pixel to vector mapping differently. We just use the same mapping for all images.

So, having said that, we are dealing with 10,000 dimensional vectors. (Each 100x100 black-and-white photo image is now a 10,000 dimensional vectors.)

We might consider using LLE or isomap, but these have two major problems (with respect to our goals).

problem 1

What LLE or isomap learn are not reusable function.

Meaning — although the function you get from LLE or isomap can map the photo images in the training data to a lower dimensional vector, it cannot a new image (that was not in the training data) to a lower dimensional vector. The function you get from LLE or isomap doesn't know what to do with a new image (not seen in the training data).

problem 2

These types of algorithms (such as LLE or isomap) usually require a distance metric in the original space.

For our example of 100x100 black-and-white photo images, which we represent as 10,000 dimensional vectors — these types of algorithms would want a distance metric for these 10,000 dimensional vectors.

Most people are probably aware of euclidean distance. And, may have seen it written as:

Or, more generally as:

For our goal (of similar images being close to each other) this distance metric (i.e., euclidean distance) doesn't work well To see why, consider this — imagine comparing an image to the extact same image, but the whole image is shifted over to the right by one pixel. Even though to us these two images as almost exactly the same — they would have a large (probably hufe) distance apart under euclidean distance. So, euclidean distance won't work.

But, the problem is more general. Having to have a distance metric in the original space (i.e., the 10,000 dimensional vector's space) is the problem.

So, what to do. Well, we don't use LLE or isomap for dimensionality reduction. And, rather than having a distance metric in the original space (i.e., the 10,000 dimensional vector's space) we provide information about whever two images (i.e., two 10,000 dimensional vector) should be considered close to each other, or far from each other. For this (close versus far) information, this could come from:

  • augmentations
  • human annotation
  • labels
  • temporal continuity

We only need pairs of images (i.e., pairs of 10,000 dimensional vector) for which we either say they are similar (and thus close) or they are not similar (and thus far).
